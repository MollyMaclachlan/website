The People Under the Doorstep

Our house has stood since Tudor times,
through wars and storms and not a scratch
has touched its white half-timbered skin
or torn the thatch of its hairline.

Older than the walls and the roof,
here before the oak in the woods,
the people under the doorstep
moved in when the first stone was laid.

The People Under the Doorstep

Our house has stood since Tudor times,
through wars and storms and not a scratch
has touched its white half-timbered skin
or torn the thatch of its hairline.

Older than the walls and the roof,
here before the oak in the woods,
the people under the doorstep
moved in when the first stone was laid.

We see them sometimes, at sunrise;
their skin is pale, their limbs are long.
They climb up to greet the day, and
flit along the walls like shadows.

Eyes red as embers, pupils slit,
they watch from the trees, lingering
like ghosts, or reflections, or a
trick of the sunbeams in the leaves.

They cannot stand the sound of clocks,
or the scrape of cars and gravel,
and they only eat the coldest
porridge, that's seen all of the night.

They show their displeasure at dusk,
in the shattering of windows,
the shaking of beds, and in the
torrents of rain and sleet and hail.

But when all is done right, we wake
and find the cleaning has been done,
and a full breakfast set out by
our neighbours under the doorstep.

Where Do All the Years Go?

Here we are, I see, back once more,
the circle turns and time retreads
all the footsteps months had passed,
naming days we've named before.

Where do you think all the years go?
Do they shrink in hungry shadows,
or simply fade in gloaming rain,
and do they echo in refrain?

Where Do All the Years Go?

Here we are, I see, back once more,
the circle turns and time retreads
all the footsteps months had passed,
naming days we've named before.

Where do you think all the years go?
Do they shrink in hungry shadows,
or simply fade in gloaming rain,
and do they echo in refrain?

It's not that time has tired out,
for old days can be just as new
when the sunlight shines as bright,
and mirrors catch the memories right.

I sing my pining songs once more,
weaving thoughts like needled threads,
and each I think will surely last
a year again; they did before.

But one day all that's preterite
will outweigh time that's still to come,
when chances glance the paths not loved,
you'll fancy sagas sewn thereof.

Never weep for idle moments
that gather air and draw a breath,
pacing life like a written word,
making sense of all those songs you've heard.

At night I read the clock once more,
and see its hands are dyed in reds,
and tell that time is not so vast,
when all its counts have chimed before.

I think I know where the years go.
They're in a box, under my bed.
Or in a room, inside my head.
There they'll stay until I want them.

I'll choose with care the stars I mourn,
and keep them few and close to me,
so when dawn lights the sky once more,
I'll see what new things the day has in store.

Every Silence is a Different Colour

There is the sort of silence that slides down a glance,
the kind that is pale blue, forming a rime on still lips,
and black as well, in the absence of all that is unsaid.

I can tell you about the simmering silence of sparks,
which reddens the gaze and hisses without a sound,
promising its violent end with every second of life.

Every Silence is a Different Colour

There is the sort of silence that slides down a glance,
the kind that is pale blue, forming a rime on still lips,
and black as well, in the absence of all that is unsaid.

I can tell you about the simmering silence of sparks,
which reddens the gaze and hisses without a sound,
promising its violent end with every second of life.

Humans have all but forgotten the yellow silence;
the silence of eyes that wait and watch in the dark;
a stillness of two kinds, opposites as much as twins.

There are those that contrast themselves, twofold;
gold and orange they are wrapped in so soft a warmth
that can only exist when white hush drapes the outdoors.

The gaps between the birdsong are green, I think,
when the wind drops and nothing moves for a time;
leaves and flowers hold still, the air paralysed.

And there is one sort of silence that is just grey;
when sounds meet the ear, but the mind is elsewhere
and finds nothing but flickering static on a screen.

Godfall

When a god is killed its carcass lingers;
beyond destruction but not decay it leaks,
carrion for the fodder and the worm-life;
as much as leeches drain more can bleed
and putrid relics answer voiceless prayers,
dreams touched by shades without design;
the flesh can die but godhood defies.

Rootprints

Swaths of tumulted earth and shredded grass
tell the passage of creaking, groaning things;
fence posts lie smashed and splintered in the mud,
barbed wire snaring the broken twigs it caught.

A hill crest boasts of pebbles and bushes,
where once it was crowned in a wreath of green;
marks in the ground show where the jewels once grew,
scars that remember the deep roots they held.

Rootprints

Swaths of tumulted earth and shredded grass
tell the passage of creaking, groaning things;
fence posts lie smashed and splintered in the mud,
barbed wire snaring the broken twigs it caught.

A hill crest boasts of pebbles and bushes,
where once it was crowned in a wreath of green;
marks in the ground show where the jewels once grew,
scars that remember the deep roots they held.

The forest has gone, marched south in the night,
over the dales and away like the wind;
the trees tore up their roots and crawled as one
off to new lands, brighter of sky and sun.

Fields were trampled, houses battered, rooves grazed,
yet nothing was hurt, not even a fly;
when morning woke, the hills were all bald, and
only rootprints tell of the crowns they wore.

Winter's Song
